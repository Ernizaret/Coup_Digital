## Strategic Observations

- **Coin dominance does not equal victory**: Claude had by far the most coins in the game, regularly sitting at 8-11 coins, yet lost. Coins are a means to an end, and Claude's inability to protect against assassination (lacking a real Contessa) was the fatal weakness.

- **The Captain is a powerful defensive card**: Claude's Captain blocked three separate steal attempts, preventing opponents from reducing the coin lead. However, the Captain's defensive value is limited to steals -- it cannot block assassinations.

- **Assassination beats economic dominance**: Grok's 3-coin assassination eliminated the game's leading player, while Claude's 7-coin coups only removed one influence card each. The Assassin's cost efficiency is a major advantage, especially in endgame scenarios.

- **Bluff stacking is detectable**: Claude's downfall came from claiming too many roles. Having already confirmed Duke and Captain, adding a Contessa claim was a bridge too far. Opponents can and should track the number of distinct role claims a player makes relative to their remaining card count.

- **Low-profile play enables endgame positioning**: Grok spent most of the game taking Income and avoiding attention. This allowed them to enter the endgame with both cards and enough coins to strike, while higher-profile players (Claude, Gemini) exhausted each other.

- **The "one-card warrior" role**: Gemini demonstrated that a player with one card can remain highly active and disruptive (blocking, taxing, couping), but their ultimate survival depends on others not targeting them with coups.
