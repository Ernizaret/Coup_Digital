## Strategic Observations

- **Recovery from early mistakes is possible**: Claude lost a card on Turn 4 to a caught bluff but went on to win the longest game in the set. Early setbacks are not deterministic -- patient play and sharp card-tracking can overcome a card deficit.

- **Card-counting is Claude's signature advantage**: Across Games 4 and 5, Claude successfully challenged based on card-counting 5 times (Turns 8 of Game 4, and Turns 16, 17, 24 of Game 5). No other AI model demonstrated this level of tracking precision. This is the single strongest differentiator in Claude's play.

- **Claiming too many different cards is detectable**: Gemini claimed Duke, Captain, and Contessa over the course of the game. With only one card remaining, Claude correctly identified that Gemini could not also have Contessa. Players should be consistent in their role claims to avoid exposing bluffs.

- **The Assassin bluff is particularly dangerous**: Both Grok (Turn 24) and ChatGPT (Turn 17) attempted to bluff Assassin and were caught because Claude tracked that all three Assassins were accounted for. Assassin is the worst card to bluff because it has only three copies (easily tracked) and the 3-coin cost means a failed bluff is both card-losing and coin-losing.

- **Endgame economic warfare**: The Claude vs ChatGPT endgame (Turns 25-33) demonstrated how Steal + Duke block can create an economic stranglehold. By stealing ChatGPT's coins and blocking Foreign Aid, Claude prevented ChatGPT from ever reaching coup range, forcing ChatGPT into a purely reactive position.

- **Patience with last-card challenges**: Claude notably chose not to challenge ChatGPT's Captain block on Turn 31, stating "Can't risk my last card on this." This discipline -- knowing when not to challenge -- is as important as knowing when to challenge.
