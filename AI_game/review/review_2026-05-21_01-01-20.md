## Strategic Observations

- **Card counting is the most powerful skill in Coup**: Claude's Turn 16 challenge is the best play across all five games. By tracking revealed cards (two Assassins visible), Claude made a certain challenge rather than a probabilistic one. This is the gold standard for challenge decisions.

- **Resource denial through stealing is devastating**: In the endgame, Claude's repeated steals from ChatGPT created an inescapable cycle. ChatGPT gained +1 from Income but lost -2 to steals, resulting in a net loss. ChatGPT could never accumulate the 7 coins needed for a coup. This strategy is far more effective than simply racing for coins via Tax.

- **Blocking steals is essential**: ChatGPT chose "Don't block" on 7 consecutive steal attempts by Claude. This was catastrophic. Even a bluffed Captain or Ambassador block would have been better than passively losing coins every turn. The risk of a challenge is worth taking when the alternative is certain slow death.

- **Exchange can rescue a bad hand**: Grok's Turn 4 Exchange transformed a useless double-Ambassador hand into a competitive Duke + Captain hand. This is the best use of Exchange seen across all five games and demonstrates that Exchange should be considered when dealt a weak opening.

- **Gemini's AI has a fundamental strategic flaw**: Four games, same result. Gemini's challenge algorithm appears to treat early Duke claims as suspicious regardless of evidence. In a game where Duke is the most commonly held card (3 copies) and the most commonly claimed action, this aggressive challenging is statistically doomed. Gemini needs recalibration to challenge less frequently, especially on Turn 1-3 claims.
