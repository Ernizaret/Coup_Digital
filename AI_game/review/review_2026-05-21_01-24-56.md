## Strategic Observations

- **The "quiet Assassin" strategy is consistently effective**: ChatGPT has now won two games with the same approach -- play Income quietly, avoid drawing attention, and strike with Assassination when opponents have weakened each other. This works because Coup is a game where the most visible player often becomes the most targeted player.

- **Unnecessary challenges lose games**: Grok lost this game by challenging when they did not need to. The turn 12 challenge against ChatGPT's Assassin was especially egregious because Grok was not even the target. Challenging from the sidelines risks a card for no direct benefit -- even if the challenge succeeds, the action is merely cancelled rather than redirected to help the challenger.

- **Card replacement tracking remains a blind spot**: Both Grok (turn 11, "You revealed Assassin and lost Contessa -- no Duke left") and Gemini (turn 5, coin-count confusion) made challenges based on incomplete or flawed reasoning about what cards opponents could hold. The card replacement mechanic means that a player's hand can change after every reveal, making it much harder to deduce their cards than these AI players assume.

- **Gemini's failure is now a confirmed pattern**: Five games, five early eliminations through failed challenges. Gemini's decision-making model is fundamentally miscalibrated for Coup. The model consistently challenges with bad reasoning (coin-count heuristics, risk/reward miscalculations) and pays the maximum price each time. Any future game involving Gemini at this calibration level will almost certainly see the same outcome.

- **Two-front wars are fatal**: Claude was caught between Grok (aggressive coups and Tax rivalry) and ChatGPT (quiet assassination threat). Players who fight on two fronts in Coup are at a severe disadvantage because each conflict costs cards and coins while the uninvolved player accumulates resources freely.
