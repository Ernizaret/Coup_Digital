## Strategic Observations

- **Card count is decisive in the endgame**: Claude's loss of a card to the incorrect challenge on Turn 8 was the turning point. Entering the head-to-head with one card versus ChatGPT's two meant Claude needed to coup twice while only surviving one coup. This arithmetic makes a one-card deficit nearly insurmountable.

- **Incorrect reasoning leads to costly challenges**: Claude's Turn 8 challenge ("I hold both Captains, so Grok cannot have one") was factually wrong. This highlights a danger in AI gameplay: confident but incorrect reasoning about hidden information. In Coup, you can never be 100% certain about another player's cards unless all copies are visible.

- **Bluffing after card loss is highly effective**: After losing the Captain on Turn 8, Claude successfully bluffed Duke (Tax) and Duke (blocking Foreign Aid) for the remainder of the game without being challenged. The AIs appear reluctant to challenge one-card players, making bluffing from a weakened position paradoxically safe.

- **Exchange can be a wasted turn**: Grok's Exchange on Turn 11 consumed a full turn and produced cards (Assassin + Contessa) that were arguably no better than what Grok had. In a tempo-driven game like Coup, spending a turn on Exchange without guaranteed improvement is costly.

- **Gemini's challenge problem is systemic**: Three games in, Gemini has challenged legitimate claims 5 times and succeeded 0 times. This is not bad luck -- it reflects an overly aggressive challenge algorithm that does not properly weight the probability that opponents are telling the truth.
