4. **Turn 23 — Claude assassinates Gemini**: Despite being caught bluffing on Turn 19, Claude came back with a genuine Assassin to eliminate Gemini. Gemini challenged again (their habit), but this time Claude really had the Assassin. Gemini lost their last card.

5. **Turn 35 — Claude catches Grok's Captain bluff**: In the final confrontation, Grok tried to steal from Claude claiming Captain. Claude used card-counting logic ("You lost a Captain and there's already a Captain in revealed cards") to challenge successfully. Grok lost Contessa, and Claude won.

## Winner Analysis

Claude won this game through adaptability and resilience:

- **Multi-card versatility**: Throughout the game, Claude credibly claimed Duke, Captain, Ambassador, and Assassin at various points. This made Claude extremely difficult to read and pin down.
- **Recovery from setback**: Claude was caught bluffing Duke on Turn 19 and lost a card, but recovered by switching to Assassin and Exchange strategies. The ability to pivot after being exposed was crucial.
- **Effective blocking**: Claude blocked Grok's steal attempts three times (Turns 5, 9, 13) with Captain and Ambassador, denying Grok coin accumulation while protecting Claude's own reserves.
- **Surgical elimination**: Claude eliminated Gemini with Assassin (Turn 23) and Grok with a successful challenge (Turn 35), both well-timed and based on sound reasoning.
- **Card-counting in the endgame**: Claude's final challenge of Grok's Captain was based on tracking revealed cards — one Captain was lost by Grok earlier, and another was in revealed cards. This left fewer Captains available, making the challenge mathematically favorable.

## Strategic Observations

- **Adaptability wins**: Claude demonstrated that switching claimed roles after being caught bluffing (Duke to Assassin/Ambassador) is a viable recovery strategy. Opponents may assume you're still bluffing, but Claude backed it up with real cards.
- **Compulsive challenging is self-destructive**: Gemini challenged 4 times across the game, losing 3. While the one successful challenge (Turn 19) was excellent, the net effect of 3 lost cards to 1 success is devastating. Selective, evidence-based challenging is far superior to habitual challenging.
- **Passivity with strong cards is worse than aggression with weak cards**: ChatGPT held two Assassins — potentially the most aggressive hand possible — and never used either. Meanwhile, Grok with Captain + Contessa was constantly attacking. Activity level correlated more with survival than hand quality in this game.
- **Card-counting endgame**: The final resolution came through card-counting. Claude tracked that two Captains were accounted for (one lost by Grok, one in revealed cards) and challenged Grok's Captain claim. This is the most reliable challenge strategy in Coup.
- **Longest game**: At 35 turns, this was the longest game in the set, largely due to the Claude-Grok stalemate where both players repeatedly stole and blocked each other. The game dragged because neither player had a clear path to 7 coins for a coup until late.
