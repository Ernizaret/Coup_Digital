## Player Breakdowns

### ChatGPT
ChatGPT played a near-perfect game. Every action was either income-generating (Tax) or threat-removing (Coup, Assassinate). The Captain blocks against both Grok's and Claude's steal attempts were consistently effective. ChatGPT never made a risky play, never bluffed unnecessarily, and never faced a successful challenge. The only potential criticism is that the late game (Turns 11-16) was slow -- taking Income instead of Tax on some turns -- but this may have been strategic caution rather than error.

### Claude
Claude had an active but ultimately unsuccessful game. The Captain reveal on Turn 4 established credibility, and the Ambassador block on Turn 9 was a legitimate and clever defensive play. However, the fatal bluff on Turn 10 -- claiming Duke with only Ambassador -- was immediately caught because the information was public (Claude had just revealed Ambassador as their sole remaining card). This was a forced error: with only Ambassador, Claude had no strong income option and was trying to stay competitive. The game's information structure made the bluff transparent.

### Grok
Grok showed both good and bad instincts. The correct challenge of Claude's Duke bluff on Turn 10 demonstrated strong logical reasoning -- Grok identified that Claude's revealed card contradicted the Duke claim. However, the earlier failed challenge of Claude's Ambassador block on Turn 9 cost Grok their Assassin, a significant loss. In the 1v1 against ChatGPT, Grok fell into the same pattern seen in the previous game: repeatedly trying to steal from ChatGPT and being blocked every time (Turns 12, 14, 16). Grok never adapted, never tried alternative actions, and was eventually assassinated.

### Gemini
Gemini was eliminated on Turn 5 -- the earliest exit across all five games reviewed. Once again, Gemini's downfall was aggressive, failed challenges. Challenging Claude's Captain on Turn 4 was unnecessary (the steal was targeting ChatGPT, not Gemini). Challenging ChatGPT's Duke on Turn 5 with one life remaining was reckless. Gemini's Contessa + Duke starting hand had defensive and income potential, but Gemini never used Tax or waited for an assassination to block. This represents the most extreme case of Gemini's challenge-addiction across the entire game set.

## Strategic Observations

- **Gemini's systematic failure**: Across all five games, Gemini consistently challenges too early, too often, and against claims that are genuine. In this game, both challenges were against players who had the claimed cards. Gemini appears to have a fundamentally miscalibrated challenge threshold -- the model treats unverified claims as suspicious regardless of context.
- **Information-based challenges**: Grok's Turn 10 challenge of Claude is a textbook example of a well-reasoned challenge. Claude had revealed Ambassador, making a Duke claim logically inconsistent. This contrasts sharply with Gemini's challenges, which were based on suspicion rather than evidence.
- **Grok's repetition problem**: For the second consecutive game, Grok fell into a loop of stealing from ChatGPT and being blocked. This pattern suggests a systematic issue with Grok's strategic adaptation -- the model fixates on one action and does not adjust even after repeated failures.
- **Duke + Captain supremacy**: ChatGPT's Duke + Captain hand proved dominant across two games (this one and the previous). The combination provides both income generation and steal defense, creating a nearly self-sufficient position. Players without these two cards struggle to compete economically.
- **Short game dynamics**: At 17 turns, this was the shortest game in the set. The speed was driven by Gemini's rapid elimination (Turn 5), Claude's elimination (Turn 10), and ChatGPT's efficient endgame. Fewer players means faster games, and early eliminations accelerate the pace significantly.
- **Bluffing with known information**: Claude's Turn 10 Duke bluff illustrates a key Coup principle: you cannot bluff a role when public information contradicts it. When your only remaining card has been revealed (even through a block), opponents know exactly what you have, making any other claim an obvious bluff. The lesson is that blocking with your only card, while immediately beneficial, exposes your hand for future turns.
