## Strategic Observations

- **The Exchange-cycling strategy can win games**: ChatGPT demonstrated that repeatedly exchanging cards creates an information fog that makes challenges extremely dangerous for opponents. Six exchanges meant no one could reliably track ChatGPT's hand.
- **Gemini's challenge problem is a defining trait**: Across all five games, Gemini is consistently the first eliminated due to aggressive, failed challenges. This is not variance -- it is a systematic strategic flaw in the Gemini model's Coup play.
- **Card counting fails after Exchanges**: Both Claude (Turn 17) and Grok (Turn 24) lost to challenges based on card counting that failed to account for Exchange-induced deck reshuffling. Once multiple Exchanges have occurred, the deck composition is unpredictable.
- **The pacifist strategy has conditions**: ChatGPT's approach worked because (a) other players targeted each other, (b) ChatGPT maintained two cards throughout, and (c) the Exchange cycling kept ChatGPT's hand fresh. This strategy would fail if opponents coordinated against the passive player.
- **Mutual destruction benefits the bystander**: Claude and Grok's exchange of Coups (Turns 12 and 14) left both at one card while ChatGPT sat at two. This dynamic -- two aggressive players destroying each other while a passive player benefits -- is a recurring theme in these games.
- **Turn order matters**: Claude's first-turn advantage (being able to Tax before anyone could challenge) contributed to an early coin lead. Gemini's last-turn-order position (fourth) meant fewer action opportunities before elimination.
