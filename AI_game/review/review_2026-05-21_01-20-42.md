## Strategic Observations

- **Don't challenge when you hold one copy**: Claude's reasoning ("I have the Captain myself, so Grok might not have one") is a common fallacy. In a 15-card deck with 3 copies of each card, holding one copy still leaves two copies available. The probability that another player has a Captain given that you hold one is lower, but far from negligible.

- **Bluffing under pressure is transparent**: Claude's Ambassador block bluff in turn 3 came immediately after losing a challenge on the same turn. Grok correctly reasoned that a player who just lost a challenge is likely bluffing a follow-up block. Desperation bluffs are often the easiest to detect.

- **Exchange is tempo-neutral**: ChatGPT's three exchanges generated no coins and no eliminations. While hand improvement has theoretical value, in practice, the tempo loss from exchanging instead of collecting Tax or stealing can be fatal. ChatGPT eventually shifted to Tax but the damage was done.

- **Coup trading favors the richer player**: The endgame between Grok and ChatGPT was a coup exchange -- ChatGPT couped Grok, then Grok couped ChatGPT back. The player with more coins entering this phase wins. Grok's Tax-based income advantage was decisive.

- **Gemini's consistent failure mode**: Across four games now, Gemini has made early failed challenges in every single game. The model appears to have a systematic bias toward challenging that does not account for the high cost of failure (losing a card) versus the relatively modest benefit of success (opponent loses a card and the action fails). This is the single most exploitable weakness in the AI player set.
