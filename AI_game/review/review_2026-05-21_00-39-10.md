7. **Turn 49 — Gemini's final failed challenge**: After Grok exchanged twice (Turns 45 and 47) to find better cards, Grok claimed Tax with Duke. Gemini challenged, lost (Grok had genuinely acquired a Duke through exchanges), and lost their last card (Ambassador). Grok won.

## Winner Analysis

Grok won through persistence, card adaptation via Exchange, and an opponent's critical error. Key factors:

- **Survived the stalemate**: Grok endured 27 turns of blocked steals against Gemini without losing composure or making a reckless play. While the strategy was repetitive (and ultimately ineffective), Grok never challenged Gemini's Ambassador block, avoiding the risk of losing their last card.
- **Exchange as a tool of reinvention**: After losing Captain to Gemini's coup on Turn 44, Grok used Exchange twice (Turns 45 and 47) to cycle through the deck. Grok found a Duke — the ideal card for the endgame — which provided 3-coin income vs. Gemini's 1-coin Income.
- **Gemini's final challenge was the winning moment**: Gemini challenged Grok's first Tax claim, not realizing Grok had genuinely acquired a Duke through the exchanges. This was the only challenge Grok faced in the endgame, and it won the game.
- **Patience over aggression**: Grok never overcommitted to a risky play. Even when steals were being blocked turn after turn, Grok did not escalate to challenges or bluffs that could have been fatal.

## Strategic Observations

- **The Steal-Ambassador stalemate is a critical flaw in AI play.** Grok attempted to steal from Gemini thirteen consecutive times, and Gemini blocked every time. Neither player adapted — Grok never changed actions or targets, and Gemini never challenged Grok's Captain claim. A human player would break this pattern far sooner, either by challenging the Ambassador block or switching to a different action entirely.
- **Exchange is the answer to stalemates.** Grok's use of Exchange after losing their Captain on Turn 44 was the key adaptation that broke the deadlock. By cycling through the deck, Grok found a Duke and gained a 3:1 economic advantage over Gemini's Income.
- **Card-counting errors can be fatal.** Claude's incorrect challenge on Turn 16 — based on a confident but wrong count of Dukes — ended their game. Card counting is powerful but requires precise tracking of all revealed cards, including those returned to the deck via Exchanges.
- **This was the longest game (49 turns) due to the stalemate.** The 27-turn Grok-Gemini loop illustrates how the game can stagnate when both players refuse to take risks. Compare to Game 4 (15 turns), where aggressive assassinations ended the game quickly.
- **Income is viable for survival but not for winning.** Gemini survived for 49 turns on mostly Income, but never gained enough economic momentum to pressure Grok before Grok adapted. One earlier Tax or Foreign Aid attempt might have given Gemini the coup money to finish Grok before the exchanges.
- **Gemini's final challenge was poorly timed.** After Grok exchanged twice, the card distribution was completely reshuffled. Challenging based on pre-exchange knowledge was a logical error. When opponents use Exchange, previous card reads become unreliable.
