About Me

I'm a physics graduate from UCLA with a big love for board games. Coup was easy enough to program and the social aspect made it interesting in a LLM context. The game offered a good blend of strategy, logical deduction, and theory of mind which made for a great environment to test the capabilities of these LLMs.

I set out to build a fully automated environment where LLMs can play coup — a game that rewards deception, reading opponents, and calculated risk-taking.

The Experiment

What Is Coup?

Coup is a social deduction card game where players bluff about which influence cards they hold to perform powerful actions. Challenge someone's claim and guess right — they lose a card. Guess wrong — you do. Last player standing wins.

It's a game of incomplete information, deception, and risk assessment — exactly the kind of challenge that tests whether AI can reason about what opponents believe, not just what they know.

Why AI vs AI?

Most AI game benchmarks focus on perfect-information games (chess, Go) or well-structured imperfect-information games (poker). Coup is different: it's a social deduction game where bluffing, trust, and reputation matter.

By pitting frontier LLMs against each other across hundreds of games, we can measure which models are best at deception, which ones challenge appropriately, and how prompt engineering (rules, strategy hints, history depth) affects play quality.

The Setup

Each game runs 4 AI players with different model + configuration combos. The game engine enforces rules, tracks cards, and feeds each player a prompt with the game state. Players respond with their chosen action and reasoning.

Configurations vary across: model (Claude, GPT-4o, Grok, Gemini, Mistral), history depth (how many past turns the AI can see), and whether rules or strategy guides are included in the prompt.

What We Measure

Beyond win rates and ELO, we track behavioral metrics: bluff success rate (how often bluffs go unchallenged), challenge accuracy (how often challenges catch real bluffs), card guess accuracy, and token efficiency.

Every game is logged turn-by-turn with full AI reasoning, and a subset have been reviewed with detailed strategic analysis.

Technical Approach

Architecture

The game engine is a Python implementation of Coup with a state-machine controller. Each AI "player" is an adapter that constructs a prompt from the current game state, sends it to the model's API, and parses the response into a valid game action.

Models Tested

Claude (Opus 4.6) GPT-4o Grok 4.3 Gemini 2.0 Flash Mistral Nemo

Prompt Engineering Variables

  • History Depth — How many previous turns are included in the prompt (0, 2, 3, 5, 10, 20). More history means more context for pattern recognition but also more tokens.
  • Rules — Whether the full game rules are included in the system prompt. Some models play better when explicitly told the rules; others do fine without them.
  • Strategy — Whether strategic guidance (when to bluff, when to challenge, economic thresholds) is included. Tests whether models benefit from being told how to think about the game.

Data Collection

Every game produces a full markdown transcript with AI reasoning visible, a CSV row with aggregate statistics per player, and turn-by-turn point tracking. Results are aggregated into model-level win rates, ELO ratings, and behavioral profiles.

Key Findings

Grok Dominates

Grok 4.3 achieves the highest ELO (1614) and best overall win rate (52%) with rules enabled. Its challenge accuracy (58%) is far above the field, suggesting superior opponent modeling.

GPT-4o: Strong Second

GPT-4o places second with an ELO of 1581 and 39% win rate (rules on, no strategy). It's the most efficient bluffer — 80% bluff success rate — but benefits heavily from having rules in the prompt.

Claude: Honest Player

Claude Opus achieves a respectable 1541 ELO but bluffs less aggressively. Its strength is consistency — solid performance across configurations without dramatic drops.

Rules Matter, Strategy Sometimes Hurts

For most models, including rules in the prompt improves performance. Strategy guidance is mixed — it helps some models but actually hurts Grok's win rate, possibly by making it too predictable.
